\begin{abstract}
\textbf{Background:} On arrival at the emergency department (ED), triage nurses record
vital signs before any chest imaging is ordered. This has motivated multimodal models
that combine the chest radiograph (CXR) with clinical context to detect pneumonia. It
remains unclear whether such fusion actually helps once the features are restricted to
what was available at the moment of prediction and the results are repeated across
training runs, because earlier studies often report a single run and omit audits for
leakage and missing data.
\textbf{Methods:} We built a cohort that respects timing by linking MIMIC-CXR-JPG,
MIMIC-IV and MIMIC-IV-ED, anchoring every feature to the moment the radiograph was
acquired ($t_0$) and admitting only measurements resolved at or before $t_0$. Splitting
by patient and by time (test $n{=}1{,}075$; prevalence 45.3\%), we trained an image
model, concatenation and attention fusion models, and clinical baselines over five
random seeds, and added an early laboratory panel as a third modality. We present the
procedure as a reusable protocol, TEMPO (time-safe, equivalence-tested,
missingness-audited, patient-level, out-of-sample). Alongside AUROC, AUPRC, expected
calibration error (ECE) and Brier score, reported as mean$\pm$SD over seeds with paired
deltas and patient-level bootstrap intervals, we test equivalence against the margin
with two one-sided tests (TOST) and report decision-curve, subgroup, interpretability
(SHAP and Grad-CAM) and external analyses on NIH ChestX-ray14, together with explicit
temporal, split and preprocessing audits.
\textbf{Results:} A fine-tuned DenseNet-121 reaches AUROC $0.737\pm0.003$, well above
the triage baselines (0.61). Adding triage vitals leaves discrimination unchanged
($\Delta$AUROC $+0.004\pm0.009$; TOST confirms equivalence to the image model against a
pre-specified $\pm0.05$ margin, with 80\% power to detect a difference of 0.010), and a
late-fusion design in the style of MedFuse behaves the same way. A calibration
advantage that looked large in one run (ECE 0.040 vs 0.067) falls to
$\Delta$ECE $-0.013\pm0.016$ once averaged over seeds, so a single run overstated it
roughly twofold. Early labs give a small but consistent gain ($+0.009\pm0.005$) that an
ablation traces entirely to which labs were ordered rather than to their values, and
that is not leakage. On external data the image model transfers with little loss (AUROC
$0.722\pm0.005$ on 112{,}120 NIH radiographs).
\textbf{Conclusion:} The radiograph carries the signal that separates pneumonia from
its absence; the clinical context available at triage does not reliably add to it, and
a naive fusion model can look helpful for reasons that will not hold up. We draw two
cautions for clinical multimodal AI: a single training run can inflate an apparent
calibration gain, and a model can lean on the pattern of which measurements are missing
and pass it off as a benefit of the added modality.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
Pneumonia is an acute lower respiratory tract infection and one of the leading reasons
for emergency department (ED) presentation, hospital admission and death worldwide
\citep{metlay2019idsa}. The frontal chest radiograph (CXR) is the recommended
first-line investigation, yet its value in the ED is limited by how quickly a
radiologist can read it during busy periods. An automated reader that returns well
calibrated probabilities is therefore appealing for decision support, and deep
convolutional networks now approach expert performance on many radiographic findings
\citep{huang2017densenet}.

An appealing but underused source of complementary information is the ED triage record.
On arrival a nurse records vital signs (temperature, heart rate, respiratory rate,
peripheral oxygen saturation (SpO$_2$), blood pressure and a pain score) together with
an acuity level, all \emph{before} imaging is ordered. Combining the radiograph with
this readily available context is widely proposed
\citep{acosta2022multimodal,hayat2022medfuse,soenksen2022multimodal}, on the intuition
that the two together should better reflect the clinical picture at the moment of
decision. The intuition mirrors how clinicians work: a physician reads the image
alongside the patient's physiology rather than in isolation. Whether a model gains the
same benefit is an empirical question, and it depends on how much information each
modality carries that the other does not.

Three recurring problems in this literature motivate our study. The first is timing:
clinical variables are often included without checking that they were actually
available when the prediction is made, which risks leaking information generated after
the decision point. The second is reliance on a single run: calibration and small
differences in discrimination are frequently reported from one training seed, even
though both are sensitive to training randomness
\citep{guo2017calibration,nixon2019measuring}. The third is missing data: clinical
inputs in the ED are missing often and not at random, so a model can learn from
\emph{whether} a measurement was taken rather than from its value, which can look like
a genuine benefit of the extra modality.

We therefore ask a deliberately conditional question: \emph{when, if ever, does the
clinical context available at triage help a CXR pneumonia model?} We answer it under a
verified timing constraint, with replication across seeds and explicit audits for
missing data and leakage, following current reporting guidance for prediction models
and medical imaging \citep{collins2024tripodai,tejani2024claim}. Rather than a new
architecture, our contribution is a careful and reproducible evaluation, validated on
external data, which we call \textbf{TEMPO} (Time-safe, Equivalence-tested,
Missingness-audited, Patient-level, Out-of-sample; Algorithm~\ref{alg:tempo}). It
adapts established reporting guidance to the particular hazards of clinical multimodal
fusion, and it yields a clear answer together with two transferable cautions:

\begin{itemize}
\item A \textbf{time-safe, reproducible multimodal ED cohort} that links three MIMIC
  databases at the radiograph acquisition time $t_0$, with every feature checked to
  precede imaging (none of 314{,}145 laboratory values fall after $t_0$).
\item An \textbf{evaluation across five seeds} of the image model, triage fusion and
  laboratory fusion, compared with a paired bootstrap, which shows that the clinical
  context available at triage does not improve discrimination.
\item A \textbf{rule of thumb for when fusion helps}: combining the radiograph with
  clinical data adds value when the target lies partly outside the image, as in
  prognosis or screening, but not when the image itself defines the label, as in
  radiographic diagnosis. This reconciles our negative result with the gains reported
  for prognostic tasks and indicates where fusion is worth the extra complexity.
\item \textbf{Caution 1 (single-run calibration inflation):} a calibration advantage
  that looks large from one training run shrinks by about half and becomes unstable
  once the run is repeated across seeds.
\item \textbf{Caution 2 (missingness as signal):} a small gain from laboratory data
  that an ablation reproduces entirely from the \emph{missingness indicators}, with no
  contribution from the measured values, and that is not leakage.
\item \textbf{External validation} of the image model on 112{,}120 NIH ChestX-ray14
  radiographs, where it generalises across institutions with an AUROC drop of only
  $0.016$, together with subgroup, decision-curve, interpretability and modern-backbone
  analyses.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Pneumonia and ED triage}
Pneumonia manifests on a frontal CXR as regions of increased opacity (consolidation)
where air-filled alveoli fill with exudate. Reported CXR sensitivity for pneumonia
is moderate (0.43--0.74) with high specificity, reflecting both the difficulty of
early detection and reliable exclusion in unambiguous cases \citep{metlay2019idsa}.
Triage vital signs quantify physiological perturbation and severity; they are highly
correlated with pneumonia but non-specific, since tachycardia, hypoxia and high
acuity accompany many respiratory presentations. Crucially, triage measurements are
recorded before imaging is ordered (a workflow guarantee, not a statistical
regularity) so they carry no risk of temporal leakage.

\subsection{The MIMIC database family}
The Medical Information Mart for Intensive Care (MIMIC) programme provides the
largest publicly available de-identified clinical dataset linked to real hospital
records from the Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center (BIDMC). This study uses three
interoperable components. \textbf{MIMIC-CXR-JPG}~\citep{johnson2019mimiccxr} contains
227{,}835 chest-radiograph studies from 64{,}588 patients (2011--2019) with CheXpert
labels for 14 findings. \textbf{MIMIC-IV}~\citep{johnson2023mimiciv} provides
hospital-admission and laboratory records, enabling temporal consistency checks and
the laboratory modality. \textbf{MIMIC-IV-ED}~\citep{johnson2023mimicived} provides
ED-specific records for all BIDMC emergency visits, including the structured triage
table used as the tabular input. All are distributed through PhysioNet under a Data
Use Agreement; per-patient date shifts preserve temporal ordering and durations.

\subsection{Automated labelling: CheXpert}
CheXpert~\citep{irvin2019chexpert} is an NLP pipeline that extracts binary labels for
14 thoracic conditions from free-text reports, assigning each a value of positive,
negative, uncertain ($-1$), or not-mentioned. The uncertain label captures hedging
language (``cannot exclude'', ``possible pneumonia''). We adopt \emph{u\_ignore}
(exclude uncertain studies) as the primary policy, which preserves label quality at
the cost of a smaller, higher-prevalence evaluation set.

\subsection{Deep learning and multimodal fusion}
DenseNet-121~\citep{huang2017densenet} propagates features through dense
connectivity, $x_\ell = H_\ell([x_0,\dots,x_{\ell-1}])$, and forms a strong CXR
backbone under transfer learning. Intermediate (feature-level) fusion extracts
modality-specific embeddings and combines them, by concatenation or by attention
over modality tokens~\citep{acosta2022multimodal}.

Prior clinical fusion studies typically report positive gains, but on tasks that
differ from ours in an important way. MedFuse fuses MIMIC clinical time-series with
CXR for ICU mortality and phenotyping~\citep{hayat2022medfuse}, and the HAIM
framework fuses imaging, tabular, text and time-series for inpatient
outcomes~\citep{soenksen2022multimodal}; both target \emph{outcomes} that are not
directly visible in the radiograph. Recent emergency-department work is similar: Lee
et al.~\citep{lee2024multimodal} fuse CXR and EHR to screen for acute heart failure
in the ED (AUROC 0.89), again a task whose label is not encoded in the image. Our
result, that fusion is discrimination-neutral for radiographic \emph{pneumonia}, is
consistent with rather than contradictory to this body of work: pneumonia is defined
\emph{by} the radiograph, so the image already contains the label and there is little
room for clinical context to add discriminative signal. This distinction, that fusion
tends to help when the target lies partly outside the image but not when the image is
essentially the target, is a useful lens on when multimodal CXR fusion should be
expected to help. Much recent work develops more elaborate fusion architectures aimed at
practical obstacles such as missing modalities and modal inconsistency, for example
DrFuse, which disentangles shared from modality-specific representations for EHR and CXR
fusion~\citep{yao2024drfuse}, and general flexible-fusion networks that handle
heterogeneous inputs~\citep{hemker2024healnet}. Our contribution is orthogonal to these:
we do not propose another fusion mechanism but ask, under a strict protocol, whether the
clinical modality carries non-redundant discriminative signal for this task in the first
place, and we show that the pattern of missingness such methods must handle can itself
masquerade as a fusion benefit.

Two further literatures frame our cautions. \emph{Informative missingness}, the
observation that missing values and their patterns correlate with the label, is well
established and is often \emph{exploited} as a predictive feature~\citep{che2018recurrent};
we demonstrate the flip side, that within a fusion model it can \emph{masquerade} as a
chemistry-driven benefit and therefore must be controlled for. \emph{Calibration under
distribution shift} is known to be fragile for single models and post-hoc scaling,
with model averaging more robust~\citep{ovadia2019trust}; this is consistent both with
our single-seed calibration caution and with the prevalence-driven degradation of ECE
we observe on the external NIH set.

To our knowledge, no prior work simultaneously (i) targets ED-specific pneumonia,
(ii) uses ED triage vitals under a verified time-safety constraint, (iii) evaluates
with a patient-level temporal split \emph{and} multi-seed replication, and (iv)
audits missingness and leakage explicitly (Table~\ref{tab:related}). This study fills
that gap.

\section{Materials and Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
The study instantiates the TEMPO protocol (Algorithm~\ref{alg:tempo}); the following
subsections detail each step, and Figure~\ref{fig:cohort_flow} shows the data flow it
produces.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{TEMPO: time-safe, equivalence-tested, missingness-audited evaluation of
clinical multimodal fusion.}
\label{alg:tempo}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require imaging $+$ multi-source clinical records; per-instance prediction time
$t_0$; equivalence margin $\Delta_0$; seed set $S$
\State Anchor each instance at $t_0$; admit a clinical feature only if its timestamp
$\le t_0$, and verify empirically that no feature post-dates $t_0$ \Comment{time-safety}
\State Split at the \emph{patient} level and temporally (order by earliest $t_0$); fit
all preprocessing on the training split only
\For{each seed $s \in S$}
  \State train the image-only and fusion models; store test predictions on identical rows
\EndFor
\State Aggregate per-seed metrics (mean\,$\pm$\,SD) and paired fusion$-$image deltas;
quantify uncertainty by a patient-level paired bootstrap
\State \textbf{Equivalence:} TOST of the paired AUROC delta against $\pm\Delta_0$;
report the effect detectable at 80\% power
\State \textbf{Missingness audit:} refit fusion with missingness \emph{indicators only}
(no values); attribute any gain to structure vs.\ measured values
\State Report calibration with multi-seed spread and bootstrap CIs; validate the
transferable branch on an external dataset
\Ensure discrimination/calibration verdict with an equivalence decision and a
missingness attribution
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Dataset description}
\label{subsec:datasets}
The task is binary radiographic-pneumonia classification of a frontal ED CXR. Each
of the three MIMIC components plays a distinct role. MIMIC-CXR-JPG supplies the
\emph{images} (JPEG radiographs) and the \emph{labels} (CheXpert pneumonia), together
with DICOM metadata (view position; StudyDate/StudyTime, from which the temporal
anchor $t_0$ is derived). MIMIC-IV-ED supplies the \emph{primary tabular modality}:
the triage table (vitals, pain, acuity) recorded at ED intake, linked to each CXR
through the ED stay. MIMIC-IV supplies the \emph{admission records} used to verify
temporal consistency and the \emph{laboratory events} used for the time-safe
laboratory modality. The image branch consumes MIMIC-CXR-JPG; the tabular branch
consumes MIMIC-IV-ED (triage) and, in the laboratory variant, MIMIC-IV (labs); the
CheXpert labels supervise all models.

\subsection{Cohort construction pipeline}
\label{subsec:cohort}
A scripted pipeline links the sources through temporally constrained relational
joins. Each CXR study is linked to a hospital admission (by \texttt{subject\_id} and
admission window) and to an ED stay (by \texttt{stay\_id} with $t_0 \in
[\text{intime}, \text{outtime}]$); one-to-one study-to-stay integrity is enforced by
deduplication. The triage table is merged on \texttt{stay\_id}; studies are
restricted to frontal (PA/AP) projections; CheXpert pneumonia labels are merged and
the \emph{u\_ignore} policy applied. The temporal anchor is $t_0 =
\text{StudyDate}+\text{StudyTime}$ from DICOM metadata. The resulting cohort
comprises 9{,}154 ED-anchored studies (Figure~\ref{fig:cohort_flow}); the
patient-level temporal split (below) assigns train 7{,}144 / validation 930 / test
1{,}080 studies. Models train and
evaluate only on radiographs present on disk in our data snapshot, yielding a
held-out test set of $n{=}1{,}075$ (487 positive / 588 negative; prevalence 45.3\%).
The entire pipeline is reproducible from a single entry point, emitting versioned
manifests at each stage. Table~\ref{tab:cohort} summarises the cohort: the split
sizes, test-set demographics (median age 65; 55\% female; predominantly White with
substantial Black and Hispanic representation), the near-even PA/AP view split, and
the triage vital-sign distributions with their missingness rates (3--8\% per vital),
which motivate the per-vital missingness indicators.

\subsection{Time-safety and the patient-level temporal split}
\label{subsec:timesafety}
Every clinical feature is either a triage measurement (recorded at intake, before
imaging by protocol) or a laboratory value with charttime in
$(t_0{-}24\,\text{h}, t_0]$. Time-safety was verified empirically on the extracted
laboratory events: \emph{zero} of 314{,}145 measurements have a charttime after
$t_0$ (median lead time 1\,h). Patients are ranked by their earliest $t_0$ and
partitioned 80/10/10 into train/validation/test, so no patient appears in more than
one split (verified: 0 cross-split subjects, 0 duplicate studies). This ordinal-time
construction approximates prospective deployment. The tabular preprocessor (median
imputation, standardisation, one-hot encoding) is fit on the training split only.

\subsection{Feature engineering}
\label{subsec:features}
\textbf{Triage features} are temperature, heart rate, respiratory rate, SpO$_2$,
systolic and diastolic blood pressure, pain score and ESI acuity, plus the
radiograph view and demographics. Physiologically implausible values are clipped to
clinical ranges. For each vital a binary \emph{missingness indicator} is added and
the value median-imputed; because ED missingness is not missing-at-random, the
indicator itself carries information. \textbf{Laboratory features} comprise 26
concepts (complete blood count, metabolic panel, liver panel, blood gas, lactate, and
the inflammatory markers CRP and procalcitonin). For each study and concept we take
the last value resolved at or before $t_0$ within the 24\,h window, and add a
missingness flag recomputed after the cohort join.

\subsection{Models}
\label{subsec:models}
Figure~\ref{fig:arch} shows the multimodal architecture.
The \textbf{image model} is DenseNet-121, initialised from ImageNet, multilabel-%
pretrained on 182{,}637 non-ED MIMIC-CXR studies (14 CheXpert findings, masked
binary cross-entropy over observed labels), then fine-tuned for binary pneumonia at
$224\times224$ with class-balanced loss and differential learning rates
($5\times10^{-5}$ head, $10^{-5}$ backbone). Multilabel pretraining minimises a masked binary cross-entropy that ignores
not-mentioned CheXpert labels,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{mBCE}} = -\frac{1}{\sum_i m_i}\sum_i m_i\bigl[y_i\log\hat p_i +
(1-y_i)\log(1-\hat p_i)\bigr],
\label{eq:mbce}
\end{equation}
where $m_i\in\{0,1\}$ masks unobserved conditions. The triage vector is encoded by a
two-layer TabularMLP (batch-normalised, dropout $0.2$) to a 128-d embedding $e_t$.
The \textbf{concatenation} model concatenates the 1024-d image embedding $e_v$ with
$e_t$, $[e_v; e_t]\in\mathbb{R}^{1152}$, and applies a fully connected head; the
\textbf{attention} model projects each embedding to a $d$-dimensional token, prepends
a learnable \texttt{CLS} token, and applies a two-layer Transformer whose scaled
dot-product attention
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Attention}(Q,K,V)=\mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V
\label{eq:attn}
\end{equation}
mixes the $[\texttt{CLS},e_v,e_t]$ tokens; the \texttt{CLS} output feeds the
classifier \citep{acosta2022multimodal}. The \textbf{$+$labs} model appends the 26 lab
values and their flags to the tabular vector. \textbf{Clinical baselines} are
logistic regression and XGBoost \citep{chen2016xgboost} on triage features. A
\textbf{late-fusion baseline} fuses at the decision level rather than the feature
level: the independently trained image and triage-logistic models each emit a
probability and a logistic meta-learner combines them, fit on the validation split, a
static analogue of the MedFuse late-fusion design~\citep{hayat2022medfuse}. A
\textbf{modern-backbone baseline} uses a CheXpert-trained DenseNet-121
\citep{cohen2022torchxrayvision} (not trained on MIMIC), zero-shot and as a
frozen-feature linear probe. All deep models share the multilabel-pretrained backbone
so the multi-seed analysis isolates fine-tuning stochasticity.

\subsection{Training and evaluation}
\label{subsec:eval}
Each deep model is trained across five random seeds (42, 123, 456, 789, 1000) with early
stopping on validation AUPRC. Primary metrics are AUROC, AUPRC, ECE and Brier score,
reported as mean$\pm$SD over seeds with paired per-seed deltas against the image-only
model on identical test rows; within-checkpoint uncertainty uses a 2{,}000-replicate
patient-level paired bootstrap (patients, not studies, are resampled). A symmetric
equivalence margin of $\pm0.05$ AUROC was pre-specified
\citep{piaggio2012noninferiority}; we chose 0.05 because a difference below it is small
relative to the seed-to-seed and cross-institution variation the image model already
shows (Table~\ref{tab:main}; a 0.016 external drop) and is unlikely to change the
operating-point behaviour a clinician would act on. Equivalence of a fusion model to the
image-only model is tested formally by two one-sided tests (TOST) against $\pm0.05$ on
the patient-level paired-bootstrap distribution of the AUROC delta, and we report the
minimal effect detectable at 80\% power. Calibration is assessed with the ECE
\begin{equation}
\text{ECE} = \sum_{b=1}^{B}\frac{n_b}{n}\,\bigl|\bar p_b - \bar y_b\bigr|,
\label{eq:ece}
\end{equation}
(with $B{=}10$ uniform bins; $\bar p_b, \bar y_b$ the mean predicted probability and
observed frequency in bin $b$), the maximum calibration error, Brier score and
reliability diagrams; ECE bin-count/scheme sensitivity is examined. Clinical utility
uses decision-curve analysis with net benefit
\begin{equation}
\text{NB}(p_t) = \frac{\text{TP}}{n} - \frac{\text{FP}}{n}\cdot\frac{p_t}{1-p_t}.
\label{eq:nb}
\end{equation}
Interpretability uses exact TreeSHAP \citep{chen2016xgboost} for the XGBoost baseline
and Grad-CAM for the image branch. We additionally compute subgroup metrics (sex,
age, race, acuity, view), operating points at fixed sensitivity, a laboratory
\emph{missingness-only} ablation, and external validation on all 112{,}120 NIH
ChestX-ray14 radiographs \citep{wang2017chestxray} with preprocessing matched to the
internal transform.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Discrimination: imaging dominates, fusion is neutral}
\label{subsec:disc}
Table~\ref{tab:main} and Figs.~\ref{fig:training}--\ref{fig:multiseed} summarise the
primary results. Both CXR models beat the triage baselines by more than 12 AUROC points, which
confirms that the radiograph carries the signal that discriminates. Adding triage
vitals does not change discrimination: across seeds the paired concat$-$image
$\Delta$AUROC is $+0.004\pm0.009$ (range $[-0.007,+0.014]$). On a paired bootstrap over
patients of the seed-averaged models the delta is $+0.006$, with a 90\% confidence
interval of $[+0.000,+0.012]$ that lies entirely inside the pre-specified $\pm0.05$
margin; both one-sided tests reject at $p<0.001$, so fusion is formally equivalent to
imaging in discrimination. Because the two models share a backbone and their
predictions are strongly correlated, the paired comparison has good power: the smallest
AUROC difference we could detect at 80\% power is $0.010$, five times below the margin,
so the null is not an artefact of low power. The result does not depend on our choice of
feature-level fusion either. A late-fusion baseline in the style of MedFuse, which
combines the separately trained image and triage models with a logistic meta-learner
fitted on the validation split, is also neutral ($\Delta$AUROC $+0.000\pm0.001$ across
seeds; TOST again confirms equivalence), even though the meta-learner gives the triage
stream a real weight. No fusion we tried, whether early or late, recovers a gain in
discrimination. Attention fusion matches the image AUROC but is badly and unevenly
calibrated (ECE $0.076\pm0.042$; Fig.~\ref{fig:deltas}), a reminder that AUROC alone is
not enough for choosing a model. The precision-recall curves (Fig.~\ref{fig:pr}) follow
the same ordering at the 45.3\% prevalence. The result also shows in the
representations: in a t-SNE of the learned embeddings (Fig.~\ref{fig:tsne}), the triage
embedding is not aligned with the label and the fused representation is essentially the
image embedding.

\paragraph{Training dynamics.} Figure~\ref{fig:training} shows the fine-tuning curves.
All three models reach their best validation AUPRC within the first one or two epochs,
which reflects the strong pretrained initialisation (ImageNet then CheXpert) and the low
backbone learning rate: little needs to change for the model to perform near its ceiling.
The curves also show where the models struggle. The image-only model overfits, its
training loss falling toward zero while validation loss climbs steadily and validation
AUPRC decays after the second epoch, so early stopping is essential. The attention fusion
model converges earliest but is the least stable: its validation loss rises sharply (to
about 2.9) and its validation AUPRC oscillates from epoch to epoch, consistent with the
poor and variable calibration it shows in Table~\ref{tab:main}. Selecting on validation
AUPRC stops each model before these failure modes take over.

Figure~\ref{fig:reliability} shows reliability diagrams for all five variants on the
held-out test set, and Table~\ref{tab:calib} reports their calibration metrics. The
multimodal-concat model is the best calibrated: its curve tracks the diagonal across the
whole probability range (ECE 0.040). The image-only model is moderately overconfident
between 0.5 and 0.8, where its curve sits below the diagonal (ECE 0.067), and the
attention fusion model is the clear outlier, sitting well above the diagonal and
systematically under-predicting (ECE 0.132). The maximum calibration error is less
reassuring: the triage baselines look well calibrated on average (ECE 0.037 to 0.046) but
carry large worst-bin errors (MCE up to 0.489), so they can be badly wrong for some
patients even when their mean calibration looks good. These differences matter in
the emergency department: an overconfident model can attach falsely reassuring low
probabilities to genuine pneumonia, while a badly under-confident model inflates apparent
risk and erodes trust in the tool, so calibration, and not discrimination alone, has to
be checked before any deployment.

\emph{Caution 1: a single run inflates the calibration effect.} The single checkpoint in
Figure~\ref{fig:reliability} suggested the concat model was markedly better calibrated
than the image model (ECE 0.040 vs 0.067, a 40\% reduction). Across seeds, however, the image-only ECE alone
ranges 0.041--0.060, and the paired advantage shrinks to $\Delta$ECE $-0.013\pm0.016$
(4/5 seeds favour concat; the range crosses zero). The single-seed estimate thus
overstated the benefit roughly twofold. Within-checkpoint uncertainty tells the same
story: patient-level bootstrap 95\% CIs on the seed-averaged ECE overlap almost
entirely (image 0.042, CI $[0.033,0.077]$; concat 0.037, CI $[0.028,0.068]$), so the
calibration gap is not resolvable even before seed variance is added. The apparent gap is also binning-dependent
(Fig.~\ref{fig:ece}): under coarser or quantile bins it shrinks further
\citep{nixon2019measuring}. Brier scores are essentially unchanged, so any effect is
distributional alignment rather than refinement. The attention fusion model's
miscalibration, in contrast, is consistent across seeds (ECE $0.076\pm0.042$, the worst
of any model), so it reflects a genuine defect of that design and not a single-run
artefact. Finally, a simple post-hoc correction removes
what remains of the gap: temperature scaling of the image model (optimal $T=1.37$,
fit on the validation logits) lowers its test ECE from 0.060 to 0.042, matching the
concat model's 0.040. The modest calibration difference therefore does not motivate
multimodal fusion on calibration grounds, since a single-parameter recalibration of
the image model alone recovers it~\citep{ovadia2019trust}.

\subsection{Caution 2: the ``labs'' benefit is missing-data structure}
\label{subsec:labs}
Appending early labs gives the only seed-consistent AUROC gain (labs$-$image
$+0.009\pm0.005$, 5/5 seeds, range excluding zero). Three analyses show this is
neither chemistry nor leakage. \emph{Ablation:} a model using laboratory
\emph{missingness flags only} (no values) reproduces the gain exactly (flags$-$image
$+0.009$; Fig.~\ref{fig:deltas}); the lab values add nothing further to
discrimination. \emph{Subcohort:} on the 269 test studies that actually have labs,
discrimination is tied (labs 0.778 vs image 0.776). \emph{Leakage audit:} 0 of
314{,}145 lab measurements post-$t_0$; 0 cross-split subjects; preprocessing fit on
train only; the ``any lab drawn'' indicator has near-null univariate AUROC (0.464).
The gain therefore reflects the weak complementary signal of \emph{whether} bloodwork
was ordered, is small, and lies within the patient-level bootstrap CI.

\subsection{Clinical availability of laboratory results}
\label{subsec:labcov}
Under time-safe matching, only about a quarter of ED-anchored CXR studies have any
resolved laboratory value at or before imaging (25.4\% of the full cohort; 269 of
the 1{,}075 test studies, 25.0\%; Fig.~\ref{fig:labcov}).
Complete-blood-count and metabolic panels reach $\sim$25\%, but the most
pneumonia-specific markers (CRP and procalcitonin) are present in only 0.5\% and
0.6\% of studies. At the moment of imaging the informative labs are essentially
unavailable, which explains both the small labs effect and its missingness origin.

\subsection{Interpretability}
\label{subsec:interp}
For the triage-only XGBoost model, exact TreeSHAP attributes the largest importance
to SpO$_2$ (mean $|\text{SHAP}|=0.148$), followed by heart rate (0.099), temperature
(0.078) and respiratory rate (0.062) (Table~\ref{tab:shap};
Fig.~\ref{fig:shap}), consistent with the pathophysiology of pneumonia, in which
alveolar consolidation impairs gas exchange. For the image branch we compare six
class-activation-mapping variants, CAM~\citep{zhou2016cam},
Grad-CAM~\citep{selvaraju2017gradcam}, Grad-CAM++~\citep{chattopadhyay2018gradcampp},
Score-CAM~\citep{wang2020scorecam}, Smooth Grad-CAM++~\citep{omeiza2019smoothgradcampp}
and XGrad-CAM~\citep{fu2020xgradcam}, on confident true positives
(Fig.~\ref{fig:gradcam}). The methods largely agree with one another and avoid the image
borders and hardware markers, so the model is not keying on obvious artefacts. Their
focus, however, sits on the central chest, the perihilar and mediastinal region together
with the medial lung, rather than tightly on the peripheral opacity a radiologist would
mark. The variants differ mainly in sharpness, with CAM, Grad-CAM, XGrad-CAM and
Grad-CAM++ the most focal and Smooth Grad-CAM++ the most diffuse, but all share this
central emphasis. This matches reports that saliency maps for chest-radiograph
classifiers often localise the abnormality poorly~\citep{arun2021saliency}: the maps show
roughly where the model looks, not a reliable outline of disease, and are better read as
a coarse sanity check than as evidence of clinically precise reasoning. The central focus
does raise the question of whether the model leans on non-pathological cues rather than
the consolidation itself, a well-documented failure mode for chest-radiograph
classifiers~\citep{zech2018variable,degrave2021shortcuts}. The strongest evidence against
a purely spurious signal here is that the image model transfers to a different institution
(AUROC 0.722 on NIH, Section~\ref{subsec:ext}), which a model relying on dataset-specific
artefacts would not do; the pneumonia-detection failure that \citet{zech2018variable}
reported was precisely a failure of cross-site transfer, so our external result speaks
directly to that concern. The imperfect localisation nonetheless remains a reason to treat
these saliency maps as weak evidence.

\subsection{Modern backbone and external validation}
\label{subsec:ext}
A CheXpert-trained DenseNet-121 \citep{cohen2022torchxrayvision}, used zero-shot
(AUROC 0.667) or as a frozen-feature linear probe (0.687), does not outperform our
task-tuned model (0.737), indicating the conclusions are not an artefact of an
outdated backbone. To test whether fusion neutrality is instead specific to the CNN
architecture or to feature freezing, we also fine-tuned a Vision Transformer
(ViT-Small/16, ImageNet-pretrained) end-to-end on the identical cohort and split
(seed 42): image-only reached AUROC 0.712 and triage-concat fusion 0.696, a change of
${-}0.015$. A contemporary transformer backbone, fully fine-tuned, therefore also
extracts no discriminative synergy from triage vitals over imaging alone, so the
neutrality holds across both backbone family and training regime.
On external validation against all 112{,}120 NIH ChestX-ray14
radiographs \citep{wang2017chestxray} (a different institution, scanner population and
label pipeline; prevalence 1.28\%), discrimination transfers with minimal loss:
AUROC $0.722\pm0.005$ across five seeds (ensemble 0.726), only 0.016 below the
internal 0.737 (Fig.~\ref{fig:external}). As expected under the large prevalence shift
($45\%\to1.3\%$), prevalence-dependent metrics degrade (AUPRC 0.030, ECE 0.44)
because probabilities are calibrated to the internal base rate; AUROC, which is
prevalence-independent, is the appropriate cross-dataset metric, and it holds despite
domain shift and NLP-derived external labels.

comparison must be read with care: those numbers are \emph{in-domain}, the
models being trained and tested on ChestX-ray14, whereas ours is a \emph{cross-dataset
transfer} from MIMIC with no adaptation, a harder setting. That our transfer AUROC lands
within this range, above two of the three in-domain baselines, indicates the image model
generalises rather than fitting one institution's distribution.

\subsection{Label-policy sensitivity}
\label{subsec:labelpol}
CheXpert uncertain labels can be excluded (\emph{u\_ignore}, our primary policy),

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
The chest radiograph carries the discriminative signal for radiographic pneumonia in
this ED cohort. Immediately available structured clinical context (triage vitals,
and even a broad early-laboratory panel) does not add reliable discrimination, and
the image model generalises externally with minimal loss. The asymmetry is
intuitive: the radiograph encodes the outcome (radiographic consolidation) almost
directly, whereas vital signs and labs encode severity and physiological perturbation
that are correlated with, but not specific to, pneumonia.

Two findings carry beyond this particular task. First, a calibration advantage that
looks large in a single run (a 40\% reduction in ECE) is roughly halved and becomes
unstable once we average over five seeds; calibration claims from one run should be
read with caution, and replication across seeds ought to be routine. Second, when
clinical inputs are missing often and not at random, a fusion model can exploit the
pattern of missingness and show a benefit that reflects neither the measured values
(the subcohort with labs present shows no effect of the values) nor leakage (the
temporal, split and preprocessing audits are clean). The ablation that keeps only the
missingness indicators makes this mechanism explicit, and to our knowledge it is
rarely reported in clinical multimodal studies. Both cautions are easy and cheap to
apply.

\paragraph{A cognitive-computation view.} Fusion is, in effect, a machine version of
clinical reasoning: it brings together different kinds of evidence, the image, the
physiological signals, and even what was and was not measured, into one judgement under
uncertainty. Seen this way, our two cautions are about how the machine reasons rather
than about bookkeeping. The first concerns how far its stated confidence can be
trusted: a calibration figure from one training run is a shaky guide, so its
uncertainty has to be measured across repeated runs before it can inform a decision.
The second concerns reasoning from absence: an ED record is missing for reasons that
are not random, and a model readily treats the presence of a measurement as evidence,
much as a clinician reads ``a test was ordered'' as informative, but it confuses that
ordering signal with the physiology it is meant to capture. Knowing when combined
evidence truly adds information, and when an apparent gain instead reflects unstable
confidence or the structure of missingness, matters for cognitively inspired decision
support well beyond this radiographic task.

\paragraph{When does fusion help?} Our result sharpens a simple rule. Fusion of the
radiograph with clinical data has worked best for prognostic and screening targets
whose ground truth is not the image itself, such as ICU mortality~\citep{hayat2022medfuse},
inpatient outcomes~\citep{soenksen2022multimodal} and screening for acute heart failure
in the ED~\citep{lee2024multimodal}, where the clinical record supplies information the
image cannot. Radiographic pneumonia is the opposite case: the label is defined by the
image, so triage vitals and early labs have little to add, and our result is what the
rule predicts. The point is not that fusion fails, but that its value depends on the
target lying partly outside the image, which is a useful guide to where fusion is worth
the extra complexity in the ED.

\paragraph{Label provenance and the ceiling on discrimination.} Two related objections
deserve a direct answer. First, the CheXpert labels come from the radiology report, so
the image is by construction close to sufficient for the label. We see this not as a
confound but as the mechanism behind our thesis: when the reference standard is read
off the image, clinical context outside the image has little room to add, and a neutral
result is what a well-posed task of this kind should give. If anything it makes the
conclusion conservative, since a label taken from the report favours the image as much
as possible. Second, the absolute AUROC (about 0.74) is moderate, which is expected:
pneumonia is one of the CheXpert findings with the lowest reader agreement, below 40\%
expert agreement in the CheXpert study~\citep{irvin2019chexpert}, so part of the ceiling
is genuine label noise rather than limited model capacity. That noise affects every
model equally and cancels in the paired comparison, so it does not weaken the
equivalence result.

\paragraph{Comparison with related work.} Earlier multimodal CXR studies typically
report gains from a single run on ICU or inpatient
cohorts~\citep{hayat2022medfuse,soenksen2022multimodal,lee2024multimodal}. We instead
study ED pneumonia under a verified timing constraint, with replication across seeds and
explicit audits, and reach a negative but adequately powered conclusion about
discrimination. Our missingness result complements the literature on informative
missingness: where GRU-D and similar models \emph{use} missingness as a predictive
feature~\citep{che2018recurrent}, we show that in a fusion model the same signal can be
mistaken for a benefit from the laboratory values, and we give a simple control,
keeping only the flags, that separates the two. Our calibration result agrees with
benchmarks showing that the uncertainty of a single model is unreliable under
distribution shift, whereas averaging several models is more robust~\citep{ovadia2019trust}.
Comparing raw numbers across different cohorts and label definitions is not informative;
the contribution is the rigour of the evaluation and the two cautions.

\paragraph{Limitations.} Training and the multimodal evaluation use a single
institution (MIMIC) and are retrospective. The external validation confirms that the
image model generalises to NIH, but it is limited to imaging: to our knowledge no
public dataset links ED triage, or early labs, to the chest radiograph, so the fusion
models themselves cannot yet be validated externally, and building such a linked
multi-site resource is an important community need. The sharp fall in
prevalence-dependent calibration on NIH (from 45\% to 1.3\%) is expected rather than a
defect: before deployment in a population with a different base rate, a simple
recalibration (temperature or Platt scaling) fitted on a small labelled sample from the
target site would restore calibrated probabilities without retraining, and our
temperature-scaling result shows that one parameter is enough internally. The subcohort
with labs present is small ($n{=}269$), but this reflects the clinical reality that
laboratory results are rarely available at the moment of the first ED radiograph
(Section~\ref{subsec:labcov}) rather than a sampling artefact, and it is the right
denominator for the question of whether labs help when they are available. The
\emph{u\_ignore} policy also raises the internal prevalence to 45.3\%, higher than a
typical ED pneumonia base rate; this is a property of the labelled evaluation subset
rather than a population estimate, and because AUROC is prevalence-independent and the
external validation probes a very different base rate (1.3\%), the discrimination
conclusion does not depend on it, while the prevalence-dependent metrics (AUPRC, PPV)
should be read at the local deployment prevalence. The NIH labels are text-mined, which
adds noise to the external estimate. Finally, we did not run an exhaustive hyperparameter
search over the fusion branch; the neutrality is nonetheless unlikely to reflect
under-tuning, since it holds across two feature-level fusion designs (concatenation and
attention), a decision-level late-fusion baseline whose meta-learner gives the triage
stream a real weight, restricted and expanded tabular feature sets (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}),
and the addition of a full early-laboratory panel, none of which recovers a discriminative
gain.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
Under a verified timing constraint and replication across seeds, combining the clinical
context available at triage with the chest radiograph does not reliably improve
pneumonia discrimination, and the one apparent gain comes from the pattern of missing
data. The image model itself is robust and generalises across institutions with an
AUROC drop of only 0.016. Beyond the negative result, the study contributes a
reproducible, time-safe multimodal ED pipeline and two reusable cautions, calibration
inflation from a single run and missingness as signal, for the growing field of
clinical multimodal AI. Building on the ViT-Small check reported here, future work
includes fine-tuning larger medical foundation models, validation across sites, and
prospective evaluation with recalibration to the local base rate.
