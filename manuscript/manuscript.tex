\begin{abstract}
\textbf{Background:} On arrival at the emergency department (ED), triage nurses record
vital signs before any chest imaging is ordered, which has motivated multimodal models
that combine the chest radiograph (CXR) with this context to detect pneumonia. Whether
fusion helps is almost always judged against a label read from the radiology report,
which is itself derived from the image. We ask a prior question: does the value of adding
clinical context depend on how the target label was defined?
\textbf{Methods:} We built a time-safe cohort by linking MIMIC-CXR-JPG, MIMIC-IV and
MIMIC-IV-ED, anchoring every feature to the moment the radiograph was acquired ($t_0$)
and admitting only measurements resolved at or before $t_0$, split by patient and by
time. For the same patients we defined two targets: a radiographic label from the
CheXpert reading of the report, and a clinical label from the ED discharge diagnosis
(ICD), which is not derived from the radiology report. Holding a fixed image model (a
five-seed DenseNet-121 ensemble) constant, we added physiology-only triage vitals by late
fusion and measured the change in discrimination against each label, with patient-clustered
bootstrap intervals, the DeLong test, and an interaction test on the difference between the
two gains. We repeated the contrast for heart failure and report the work as
a reusable protocol, TEMPO (time-safe, equivalence-tested, missingness-audited,
patient-level, out-of-sample).
\textbf{Results:} The two labels give opposite answers. Adding triage vitals leaves the
radiographic label unchanged (AUROC $0.743\rightarrow0.746$; $\Delta+0.003$, DeLong
$p{=}0.49$) yet raises the clinical label (AUROC $0.784\rightarrow0.808$; $\Delta+0.024$,
95\% CI $[0.009,0.039]$, DeLong $p{=}0.0015$). The gap between the two gains is itself
significant (interaction $+0.021$, 95\% CI $[0.007,0.036]$, $p{=}0.002$). The same pattern
appears for heart failure, though with only 54 clinical positives it is directionally
consistent rather than firmly powered.
A pairwise decomposition shows the vitals are redundant for the radiographic sign but
complementary for the clinical diagnosis, and the gain is carried by fever and triage
acuity, signals the radiograph does not contain. The dissociation is present across all
five image seeds, survives changes to the diagnosis code set, and is not explained by
demographics. Two cautions from the same cohort: a calibration advantage that looked
large in one run (ECE 0.040 vs 0.067) shrinks to $\Delta$ECE $-0.013\pm0.016$ across
seeds, and a fusion model can lean on which measurements are missing rather than on their
values.
\textbf{Conclusion:} Whether clinical context helps a chest-radiograph model depends on
the provenance of the label it is scored against. A target read from the image makes the
same triage information look useless, while a clinically defined target makes it valuable.
Studies of multimodal fusion should state where their label comes from, because that
choice can decide the result.

Emergency department triage \and Label provenance \and Redundancy and complementarity \and

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
Pneumonia is an acute lower respiratory tract infection and one of the leading reasons
for emergency department (ED) presentation, hospital admission and death worldwide
\citep{metlay2019idsa}. The frontal chest radiograph (CXR) is the recommended
first-line investigation, yet its value in the ED is limited by how quickly a
radiologist can read it during busy periods. An automated reader that returns well
calibrated probabilities is therefore appealing for decision support, and deep
convolutional networks now approach expert performance on many radiographic findings
\citep{huang2017densenet}.

A readily available source of context is the ED triage record. On arrival a nurse records
vital signs (temperature, heart rate, respiratory rate, peripheral oxygen saturation
(SpO$_2$), blood pressure and a pain score) together with an acuity level, all
\emph{before} imaging is ordered. Combining the radiograph with this context is widely
proposed and reviewed
\citep{acosta2022multimodal,huang2020fusion,hayat2022medfuse,soenksen2022multimodal}, on
the intuition that a model, like a clinician, should read the image alongside the patient's
physiology. Whether the model actually gains from this depends on how much the vitals
carry that the image does not, and, we argue, on a step that is easy to overlook: the
label the model is scored against.

Most studies of CXR pneumonia fusion measure success against a label taken from the
radiology report, produced by a text pipeline such as CheXpert \citep{irvin2019chexpert}.
That label is a reading of the same image the model already sees, so asking whether triage
vitals add to it comes close to asking whether physiology can improve on the radiograph's
own account of itself. A pneumonia diagnosis in the ED is more than a radiographic
pattern. It is a clinical judgement that also rests on fever, respiratory distress and
overall acuity, and it is recorded separately in the discharge diagnosis. Whether adding
clinical context helps may therefore turn on which of these targets a study happens to
use, a choice that is rarely stated and, to our knowledge, never tested directly.

Three further hazards make fusion results hard to trust, and audits of medical-imaging
machine learning keep tracing failures back to them
\citep{roberts2021pitfalls,varoquaux2022failures}; we control for all three. Timing:
clinical variables are often included without checking that they were available when the
prediction is made, which risks leaking information from after the decision point, a
leading cause of irreproducible results \citep{kapoor2023leakage}. Repetition: calibration
and small differences in discrimination are usually reported from one training run, though
both move with the random seed \citep{guo2017calibration,nixon2019measuring}. Missing data:
ED inputs are missing often and not at random, so a model can learn from \emph{whether} a
measurement was taken rather than from its value, which can look like a benefit of the added
modality.

We therefore ask whether the value of triage context depends on the provenance of the
target label, and we answer under a verified timing constraint with replication across
seeds and explicit audits for missing data and leakage, following current reporting
guidance for prediction models and medical imaging
\citep{collins2024tripodai,tejani2024claim}. For the same patients we define two targets:
a radiographic label from the CheXpert reading of the report, and a clinical label from
the ED discharge diagnosis (ICD), which is assigned without reference to the image.
Holding a fixed image model constant, we add physiology-only triage vitals by late fusion
and measure the change in discrimination against each label. We package the evaluation as
a reusable protocol, \textbf{TEMPO} (Time-safe, Equivalence-tested, Missingness-audited,
Patient-level, Out-of-sample; Algorithm~\ref{alg:tempo}). Our contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
\item A \textbf{dissociation by label provenance.} Adding the same triage vitals to a
  fixed image model leaves the radiographic label unchanged (AUROC $0.743\rightarrow0.746$,
  $\Delta+0.003$, DeLong $p{=}0.49$) but improves the clinical label
  ($0.784\rightarrow0.808$, $\Delta+0.024$, 95\% CI $[0.009,0.039]$). The difference
  between the two gains is itself significant (interaction $+0.021$, 95\% CI
  $[0.007,0.036]$, $p{=}0.002$).
\item A \textbf{mechanism.} A pairwise decomposition shows the vitals are redundant for
  the radiographic sign but complementary for the clinical diagnosis, and the gain is
  carried by fever and triage acuity, signals a radiographic-trained model does not capture.
  An image model retrained on the clinical label recovers a comparable amount, so triage is
  one route to the missing signal rather than the only one.
\item \textbf{Generality.} The pneumonia dissociation survives changes to the diagnosis
  code set, holds across all five image seeds, and is not explained by demographics; the
  same pattern appears for heart failure, though with far fewer clinical positives (54) that
  condition is directionally consistent rather than firmly powered.
\item A \textbf{methodological caution}: label provenance can decide whether multimodal
  fusion appears to help, so studies should state where their label comes from. A target
  read from the image can hide the value of context that a clinically defined target reveals.
\item A \textbf{time-safe, reproducible ED cohort} linking three MIMIC databases at the
  radiograph acquisition time $t_0$ (none of 314{,}145 laboratory values fall after
  $t_0$), with two further cautions from the same data: a single training run can inflate
  an apparent calibration gain, and a fusion model can lean on which measurements are
  missing rather than on their values.
\item \textbf{External validation} of the image model on 112{,}120 NIH ChestX-ray14
  radiographs, where it generalises across institutions with an AUROC drop of only
  $0.016$.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Pneumonia and ED triage}
Pneumonia manifests on a frontal CXR as regions of increased opacity (consolidation)
where air-filled alveoli fill with exudate. Reported CXR sensitivity for pneumonia
is moderate (0.43--0.74) with high specificity, reflecting both the difficulty of
early detection and reliable exclusion in unambiguous cases \citep{metlay2019idsa}.
Triage vital signs quantify physiological perturbation and severity; they are highly
correlated with pneumonia but non-specific, since tachycardia, hypoxia and high
acuity accompany many respiratory presentations. Crucially, triage measurements are
recorded before imaging is ordered (a workflow guarantee, not a statistical
regularity) so they carry no risk of temporal leakage.

\subsection{The MIMIC database family}
The Medical Information Mart for Intensive Care (MIMIC) programme provides the
largest publicly available de-identified clinical dataset linked to real hospital
records from the Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center (BIDMC). This study uses three
interoperable components. \textbf{MIMIC-CXR-JPG}~\citep{johnson2019mimiccxr} contains
227{,}835 chest-radiograph studies from 64{,}588 patients (2011--2019) with CheXpert
labels for 14 findings. \textbf{MIMIC-IV}~\citep{johnson2023mimiciv} provides
hospital-admission and laboratory records, enabling temporal consistency checks and
the laboratory modality. \textbf{MIMIC-IV-ED}~\citep{johnson2023mimicived} provides
ED-specific records for all BIDMC emergency visits, including the structured triage
table used as the tabular input. All are distributed through PhysioNet under a Data
Use Agreement; per-patient date shifts preserve temporal ordering and durations.

\subsection{Automated labelling: CheXpert}
CheXpert~\citep{irvin2019chexpert} is an NLP pipeline that extracts binary labels for
14 thoracic conditions from free-text reports, assigning each a value of positive,
negative, uncertain ($-1$), or not-mentioned. The uncertain label captures hedging
language (``cannot exclude'', ``possible pneumonia''). We adopt \emph{u\_ignore}
(exclude uncertain studies) as the primary policy, which preserves label quality at
the cost of a smaller, higher-prevalence evaluation set.

\subsection{Deep learning and multimodal fusion}
DenseNet-121~\citep{huang2017densenet} propagates features through dense
connectivity, $x_\ell = H_\ell([x_0,\dots,x_{\ell-1}])$, and forms a strong CXR
backbone under transfer learning. Intermediate (feature-level) fusion extracts
modality-specific embeddings and combines them, by concatenation or by attention
over modality tokens~\citep{acosta2022multimodal}.

Prior clinical fusion studies typically report positive gains, but on tasks that
differ from ours in an important way. MedFuse fuses MIMIC clinical time-series with
CXR for ICU mortality and phenotyping~\citep{hayat2022medfuse}, and the HAIM
framework fuses imaging, tabular, text and time-series for inpatient
outcomes~\citep{soenksen2022multimodal}; both target \emph{outcomes} that are not
directly visible in the radiograph. Recent emergency-department work is similar: Lee
et al.~\citep{lee2024multimodal} fuse CXR and EHR to screen for acute heart failure
in the ED (AUROC 0.89), again a task whose label is not encoded in the image. A recent
benchmark, CareBench, sharpens the pattern, reporting that EHR and CXR fusion helps most
for phenotypes that need complementary information from both sources~\citep{yin2026multimodal}.
These studies vary the task and the architecture; what none of them isolates is the target
label. Our contribution is to hold the patients, features and image model fixed and change
only the label, from the radiographic reading to the clinical diagnosis, and to show that
this single change flips the conclusion.

The premise that the label matters rests on a documented discrepancy. VisualCheXbert
reports that radiologists labelling free-text reports disagree substantially with
radiologists labelling the corresponding images, so a report-derived label is an imperfect
proxy for what is on the film~\citep{jain2021visualchexbert}. That work treats the
discrepancy as a labelling problem to be corrected; we take it as an experimental variable
and ask what it does to multimodal fusion. When the target is the report-derived
radiographic sign, the radiograph already accounts for the label and physiology has little
to add. When the target is the clinical diagnosis, which is recorded independently of the
image and rests partly on physiology, the same triage vitals become useful. Much recent
work instead develops more elaborate fusion architectures for practical obstacles such as
missing modalities and modal inconsistency, for example DrFuse~\citep{yao2024drfuse} and
flexible-fusion networks for heterogeneous inputs~\citep{hemker2024healnet}. Our
contribution is orthogonal: we do not propose another fusion mechanism but show that
whether the clinical modality carries non-redundant signal, and therefore whether any such
mechanism can help, depends on where the label comes from. We also show that the pattern of
missingness these methods must handle can itself masquerade as a fusion benefit.

Two further literatures frame our cautions. \emph{Informative missingness}, the
observation that missing values and their patterns correlate with the label, is well
established and is often \emph{exploited} as a predictive feature~\citep{che2018recurrent};
we demonstrate the flip side, that within a fusion model it can \emph{masquerade} as a
chemistry-driven benefit and therefore must be controlled for. \emph{Calibration under
distribution shift} is known to be fragile for single models and post-hoc scaling,
with model averaging more robust~\citep{ovadia2019trust}; this is consistent both with
our single-seed calibration caution and with the prevalence-driven degradation of ECE
we observe on the external NIH set.

To our knowledge, no prior work simultaneously (i) targets ED-specific pneumonia,
(ii) uses ED triage vitals under a verified time-safety constraint, (iii) contrasts a
radiographic and a clinically defined label for the same patients and image model, and
(iv) evaluates with a patient-level temporal split, multi-seed replication and explicit
missingness and leakage audits (Table~\ref{tab:related}). This study fills that gap.

\section{Materials and Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
The study instantiates the TEMPO protocol (Algorithm~\ref{alg:tempo}); the following
subsections detail each step, and Figure~\ref{fig:cohort_flow} shows the data flow it
produces.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{TEMPO: time-safe, equivalence-tested, missingness-audited evaluation of
clinical multimodal fusion.}
\label{alg:tempo}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require imaging $+$ multi-source clinical records; per-instance prediction time
$t_0$; equivalence margin $\Delta_0$; seed set $S$
\State Anchor each instance at $t_0$; admit a clinical feature only if its timestamp
$\le t_0$, and verify empirically that no feature post-dates $t_0$ \Comment{time-safety}
\State Split at the \emph{patient} level and temporally (order by earliest $t_0$); fit
all preprocessing on the training split only
\For{each seed $s \in S$}
  \State train the image-only and fusion models; store test predictions on identical rows
\EndFor
\State Aggregate per-seed metrics (mean\,$\pm$\,SD) and paired fusion$-$image deltas;
quantify uncertainty by a patient-level paired bootstrap
\State \textbf{Equivalence:} TOST of the paired AUROC delta against $\pm\Delta_0$;
report the effect detectable at 80\% power
\State \textbf{Missingness audit:} refit fusion with missingness \emph{indicators only}
(no values); attribute any gain to structure vs.\ measured values
\State Report calibration with multi-seed spread and bootstrap CIs; validate the
transferable branch on an external dataset
\Ensure discrimination/calibration verdict with an equivalence decision and a
missingness attribution
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Dataset description}
\label{subsec:datasets}
The task is binary radiographic-pneumonia classification of a frontal ED CXR. Each
of the three MIMIC components plays a distinct role. MIMIC-CXR-JPG supplies the
\emph{images} (JPEG radiographs) and the \emph{labels} (CheXpert pneumonia), together
with DICOM metadata (view position; StudyDate/StudyTime, from which the temporal
anchor $t_0$ is derived). MIMIC-IV-ED supplies the \emph{primary tabular modality}:
the triage table (vitals, pain, acuity) recorded at ED intake, linked to each CXR
through the ED stay. MIMIC-IV supplies the \emph{admission records} used to verify
temporal consistency and the \emph{laboratory events} used for the time-safe
laboratory modality. The image branch consumes MIMIC-CXR-JPG; the tabular branch
consumes MIMIC-IV-ED (triage) and, in the laboratory variant, MIMIC-IV (labs); the
CheXpert labels supervise all models.

\subsection{Cohort construction pipeline}
\label{subsec:cohort}
A scripted pipeline links the sources through temporally constrained relational
joins. Each CXR study is linked to a hospital admission (by \texttt{subject\_id} and
admission window) and to an ED stay (by \texttt{stay\_id} with $t_0 \in
[\text{intime}, \text{outtime}]$); one-to-one study-to-stay integrity is enforced by
deduplication. The triage table is merged on \texttt{stay\_id}; studies are
restricted to frontal (PA/AP) projections; CheXpert pneumonia labels are merged and
the \emph{u\_ignore} policy applied. The temporal anchor is $t_0 =
\text{StudyDate}+\text{StudyTime}$ from DICOM metadata. The resulting cohort
comprises 9{,}154 ED-anchored studies (Figure~\ref{fig:cohort_flow}); the
patient-level temporal split (below) assigns train 7{,}144 / validation 930 / test
1{,}080 studies. Models train and
evaluate only on radiographs present on disk in our data snapshot, yielding a
held-out test set of $n{=}1{,}075$ (487 positive / 588 negative; prevalence 45.3\%).
The entire pipeline is reproducible from a single entry point, emitting versioned
manifests at each stage. Table~\ref{tab:cohort} summarises the cohort: the split
sizes, test-set demographics (median age 65; 55\% female; predominantly White with
substantial Black and Hispanic representation), the near-even PA/AP view split, and
the triage vital-sign distributions with their missingness rates (3--8\% per vital),
which motivate the per-vital missingness indicators.

\subsection{Time-safety and the patient-level temporal split}
\label{subsec:timesafety}
Every clinical feature is either a triage measurement (recorded at intake, before
imaging by protocol) or a laboratory value with charttime in
$(t_0{-}24\,\text{h}, t_0]$. Time-safety was verified empirically on the extracted
laboratory events: \emph{zero} of 314{,}145 measurements have a charttime after
$t_0$ (median lead time 1\,h). Patients are ranked by their earliest $t_0$ and
partitioned 80/10/10 into train/validation/test, so no patient appears in more than
one split (verified: 0 cross-split subjects, 0 duplicate studies). This ordinal-time
construction approximates prospective deployment. The tabular preprocessor (median
imputation, standardisation, one-hot encoding) is fit on the training split only.

\subsection{Feature engineering}
\label{subsec:features}
\textbf{Triage features} are temperature, heart rate, respiratory rate, SpO$_2$,
systolic and diastolic blood pressure, pain score and ESI acuity, plus the
radiograph view and demographics. Physiologically implausible values are clipped to
clinical ranges. For each vital a binary \emph{missingness indicator} is added and
the value median-imputed; because ED missingness is not missing-at-random, the
indicator itself carries information. \textbf{Laboratory features} comprise 26
concepts (complete blood count, metabolic panel, liver panel, blood gas, lactate, and
the inflammatory markers CRP and procalcitonin). For each study and concept we take
the last value resolved at or before $t_0$ within the 24\,h window, and add a
missingness flag recomputed after the cohort join.

\subsection{Models}
\label{subsec:models}
Figure~\ref{fig:arch} shows the multimodal architecture.
The \textbf{image model} is DenseNet-121, initialised from ImageNet, multilabel-%
pretrained on 182{,}637 non-ED MIMIC-CXR studies (14 CheXpert findings, masked
binary cross-entropy over observed labels), then fine-tuned for binary pneumonia at
$224\times224$ with class-balanced loss and differential learning rates
($5\times10^{-5}$ head, $10^{-5}$ backbone). Multilabel pretraining minimises a masked binary cross-entropy that ignores
not-mentioned CheXpert labels,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{mBCE}} = -\frac{1}{\sum_i m_i}\sum_i m_i\bigl[y_i\log\hat p_i +
(1-y_i)\log(1-\hat p_i)\bigr],
\label{eq:mbce}
\end{equation}
where $m_i\in\{0,1\}$ masks unobserved conditions. The triage vector is encoded by a
two-layer TabularMLP (batch-normalised, dropout $0.2$) to a 128-d embedding $e_t$.
The \textbf{concatenation} model concatenates the 1024-d image embedding $e_v$ with
$e_t$, $[e_v; e_t]\in\mathbb{R}^{1152}$, and applies a fully connected head; the
\textbf{attention} model projects each embedding to a $d$-dimensional token, prepends
a learnable \texttt{CLS} token, and applies a two-layer Transformer whose scaled
dot-product attention
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Attention}(Q,K,V)=\mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V
\label{eq:attn}
\end{equation}
mixes the $[\texttt{CLS},e_v,e_t]$ tokens; the \texttt{CLS} output feeds the
classifier \citep{acosta2022multimodal}. The \textbf{$+$labs} model appends the 26 lab
values and their flags to the tabular vector. \textbf{Clinical baselines} are
logistic regression and XGBoost \citep{chen2016xgboost} on triage features. A
\textbf{late-fusion baseline} fuses at the decision level rather than the feature
level: the independently trained image and triage-logistic models each emit a
probability and a logistic meta-learner combines them, fit on the validation split, a
static analogue of the MedFuse late-fusion design~\citep{hayat2022medfuse}. A
\textbf{modern-backbone baseline} uses a CheXpert-trained DenseNet-121
\citep{cohen2022torchxrayvision} (not trained on MIMIC), zero-shot and as a
frozen-feature linear probe. All deep models share the multilabel-pretrained backbone
so the multi-seed analysis isolates fine-tuning stochasticity.

\caption{Multimodal pipeline, from inputs to prediction. Both inputs are anchored at the

\subsection{Two target labels and the fusion contrast}
\label{subsec:twolabels}
The central experiment holds the image model fixed and changes only the label it is scored
against. The \textbf{radiographic label} is the CheXpert reading of the report
(Section~\ref{sec:background}), a description of the film. The \textbf{clinical label} is
taken from the MIMIC-IV-ED discharge diagnosis: a study is positive if the ED stay carries
an ICD code for pneumonia (ICD-10 J12--J18 or J69.0, ICD-9 480--486 or 507.0), keyed on
\texttt{stay\_id}. It is taken from the coded discharge diagnosis rather than the radiology
report, so unlike the CheXpert label it is not read from the image. It covers 99.7\% of the
cohort. The two labels agree only moderately: clinical prevalence in the test set is
19\% against 45\% radiographic, and 36\% of CheXpert-positive studies are ICD-positive, so
they are not interchangeable targets.

To isolate the label as the only moving part we use a controlled late fusion. The image
signal is the five-seed image ensemble, held fixed. The clinical signal is a logistic model
on the physiological triage vitals alone; race and sex are excluded, because they carry no
signal here (race-only AUROC 0.52) and using them to predict a diagnosis would introduce a
fairness confound. A logistic meta-learner combines the two probabilities, fit on the
validation split, and is evaluated against each label on the identical test rows. For each
label we report the fusion-minus-image change in AUROC with a 2{,}000-replicate
patient-clustered bootstrap and the DeLong test. The dissociation itself is tested directly
with an \emph{interaction} test: on each bootstrap draw we compute both the clinical and the
radiographic delta on the same resampled patients and take their difference, so its 95\%
interval measures whether the clinical gain genuinely exceeds the radiographic one. The two
deltas are rank statistics on the same patient sample, so differencing them is well defined
despite the labels differing in prevalence. The confirmatory family is small and
pre-specified (two conditions, each with a radiographic, a clinical and an interaction
contrast); the pneumonia contrasts survive a Benjamini-Hochberg correction, whereas the
heart-failure interaction does not and is reported as directionally consistent.

We check that the pattern is not specific to pneumonia by repeating the contrast for heart
failure, pairing the multilabel model's Edema score (its radiographic finding) with the ED
diagnosis of heart failure (ICD I50, 428). We also test robustness to the diagnosis code
set, decompose the change into a pairwise redundancy-versus-complementarity account, read
off which vitals drive each label from the triage-model coefficients, and ask whether adding
pre-imaging chief-complaint symptom flags enlarges the clinical gain. The ED diagnosis codes
the presenting complaint, which limits the contrast to conditions common enough at
presentation; pneumonia and heart failure qualify, and a broader survey is left to future
work.

\subsection{Training and evaluation}
\label{subsec:eval}
Each deep model is trained across five random seeds (42, 123, 456, 789, 1000) with early
stopping on validation AUPRC. Primary metrics are AUROC, AUPRC, ECE and Brier score,
reported as mean$\pm$SD over seeds with paired per-seed deltas against the image-only
model on identical test rows; within-checkpoint uncertainty uses a 2{,}000-replicate
patient-level paired bootstrap (patients, not studies, are resampled). A symmetric
equivalence margin of $\pm0.05$ AUROC was pre-specified
\citep{piaggio2012noninferiority,lakens2017equivalence}; we take 0.05 as a conservative
upper bound on a change that could plausibly alter a clinical decision at the operating
points we consider. We acknowledge this is a generous margin, and therefore also report
the minimal detectable effect so the reader can judge the test's resolution independently
of the margin. Equivalence of a fusion model to the image-only model is tested by two
one-sided tests (TOST) against $\pm0.05$; to reflect the uncertainty a single deployed
model would face, the delta distribution and the 80\%-power minimal detectable effect are
estimated by a \emph{hierarchical} bootstrap that resamples both the training seed and the
patients (rather than the seed-averaged ensemble, which would understate the variance). Calibration is assessed with the ECE
\begin{equation}
\text{ECE} = \sum_{b=1}^{B}\frac{n_b}{n}\,\bigl|\bar p_b - \bar y_b\bigr|,
\label{eq:ece}
\end{equation}
(with $B{=}10$ uniform bins; $\bar p_b, \bar y_b$ the mean predicted probability and
observed frequency in bin $b$), the maximum calibration error, Brier score and
reliability diagrams; ECE bin-count/scheme sensitivity is examined. Clinical utility
uses decision-curve analysis with net benefit
\begin{equation}
\text{NB}(p_t) = \frac{\text{TP}}{n} - \frac{\text{FP}}{n}\cdot\frac{p_t}{1-p_t}.
\label{eq:nb}
\end{equation}
Interpretability uses exact TreeSHAP \citep{chen2016xgboost} for the XGBoost baseline
and Grad-CAM for the image branch. We additionally compute subgroup metrics (sex,
age, race, acuity, view), operating points at fixed sensitivity, a laboratory
\emph{missingness-only} ablation, and external validation on all 112{,}120 NIH
ChestX-ray14 radiographs \citep{wang2017chestxray} with preprocessing matched to the
internal transform.

\subsection{The dissociation: fusion value depends on label provenance}
\label{subsec:dissociation}
The same triage vitals, added to the same image model, give opposite answers depending on
which label they are scored against (Fig.~\ref{fig:forest}, Table~\ref{tab:dissociation}).
Against the radiographic CheXpert label the image ensemble reaches AUROC 0.743 and fusion
moves it to 0.746, a change of $+0.003$ (95\% CI $[-0.005,+0.011]$; DeLong $p{=}0.49$) that
is indistinguishable from no effect. Against the clinical ICD label the same image ensemble
scores 0.784, the triage vitals alone reach 0.703, and fusion raises discrimination to
0.808, a change of $+0.024$ (95\% CI $[0.009,0.039]$; DeLong $p{=}0.0015$). The gap between
the two gains is the effect of interest, and it is significant on its own: resampling
patients and taking the difference of the two deltas gives an interaction of $+0.021$ (95\%
CI $[0.007,0.036]$, $p{=}0.002$). A model that looks useless for the radiographic sign is
useful for the clinical diagnosis, and the difference is not attributable to sampling noise.
The image model in fact scores a little higher against the clinical label (0.784) than
against the radiographic one it was trained on (0.743); this reflects the lower prevalence
and cleaner definition of the coded diagnosis rather than the image being sufficient for it,
and it is the fusion increment, not the absolute level, that separates the two labels.

The same pattern appears for heart failure, with the multilabel model's Edema score as the
image signal: fusion is flat against the radiographic Edema label ($+0.000$, 95\% CI
$[-0.002,+0.002]$) and positive against the clinical heart-failure diagnosis ($+0.021$, 95\%
CI $[0.001,0.044]$), interaction $+0.021$ (95\% CI $[0.0004,0.044]$, $p{=}0.02$). With only
54 clinical positives this condition is underpowered, so we read it as directionally
consistent rather than a firm replication. The pneumonia gain, by contrast, survives the
choices a sceptical reader would question. It holds across all five image seeds (per-seed
clinical $\Delta$ $0.027\pm0.002$, radiographic $\Delta$ $0.004\pm0.001$), it is stable when
the code set is narrowed to exclude aspiration or widened to add influenza ($\Delta$ from
$+0.026$ to $+0.035$, all $p<0.001$), and it is not demographic: race alone predicts neither
label above chance (AUROC 0.52) and adds no fusion gain.

\subsection{Mechanism: what the vitals add, and where}
\label{subsec:mechanism}
Two analyses explain why the clinical label rewards the vitals the radiographic label
ignores. The first reads the triage-model coefficients for each label
(Fig.~\ref{fig:mechanism}). The vitals that gain weight for the clinical label are the
systemic signs of infection: the standardized coefficient on temperature rises from $0.11$
for the radiographic label to $0.30$ for the clinical one, and triage acuity from near zero
($-0.02$) to $0.20$. Fever and overall illness severity help identify the diagnosis but are
invisible as a radiographic opacity, so they add nothing when the target is the film.

The second decomposes the AUROC change itself (Fig.~\ref{fig:complementarity}). Over all
positive/negative case pairs we count how many the vitals reorder correctly, pairs the image
ranks wrongly that fusion fixes, against how many they disturb, pairs the image ranks
correctly that fusion breaks. For the radiographic label the two nearly cancel: the vitals
fix 1.1 pairs for each one they break, a redundant contribution that nets $+0.003$. For the
clinical label they fix 1.71 for each one they break, a genuinely complementary contribution
that nets $+0.024$. The gain concentrates where the image is least certain: within the middle
tertile of image scores, fusion adds $0.126$ AUROC for the clinical label against $0.016$ for
the radiographic one.

Enriching the clinical stream sharpens the same effect. Adding six pre-imaging symptom flags
parsed from the triage chief complaint (cough, dyspnoea, fever, chest pain, sputum,
respiratory distress) to the vitals leaves the radiographic label neutral ($+0.005$, 95\% CI
$[-0.004,+0.014]$) and raises the clinical gain to $+0.031$ (95\% CI $[+0.010,+0.053]$). The
flags are recorded before imaging and encode symptoms rather than a suspected diagnosis, so
they add clinical context without leaking the label.

The clinical gain also tracks the observed vitals, as a real contribution should. Masking an
increasing fraction of the triage measurements at test time, so that the model falls back on
median imputation and the missingness flags, erodes the clinical gain smoothly to zero as the
vitals disappear (Fig.~\ref{fig:missingness}). The radiographic label never benefits, and
under partial missingness fusion slightly hurts it. This is the signature of a genuine
contribution from the measured physiology rather than an artefact of the fusion step, and it
is the constructive counterpart to the missingness caution below: the same flags that can
masquerade as a benefit for one label carry a real one for the other only when the values
behind them are present.

\subsection{The clinical signal is reachable two ways}
\label{subsec:earlyfusion}
The late-fusion contrast holds the image model fixed, so the triage gain measures what a
radiographic-trained model leaves unextracted. This raises a question the controlled design
cannot answer: would an image model trained directly on the clinical label capture that signal
by itself? It largely does. Retraining the image model end to end on the ICD label raises its
test AUROC from 0.784 to 0.806 (seed 42), close to the 0.808 the fixed model reaches once
triage is added by late fusion. Adding triage to this clinical-trained model by early
(concatenation) fusion does not improve it further ($0.806\rightarrow0.799$, $\Delta-0.008$,
95\% CI $[-0.027,+0.014]$), and the radiographic label stays neutral under the same
architecture ($\Delta-0.003$). The extra clinical signal that a radiographic-trained model
misses is therefore recoverable either by adding triage vitals or by retraining the image
model on the clinical label; the two are largely substitutes rather than additive. This
locates the effect precisely. The dissociation is a property of the target label, and the
triage contribution it exposes is specific to a fixed image model that was not optimized for
that target, not a claim that physiology carries information no image model could. Notably the
clinical label is itself more predictable from the image than the radiographic label is (0.806
against 0.739), so its signal is not outside the radiograph, only outside the
radiographic-trained model. These are single-seed runs and we read them as locating the effect
rather than as a second multi-seed estimate.

\subsection{Discrimination against the radiographic label}
\label{subsec:disc}
We examine the radiographic label with the same multi-seed rigor the literature applies to
it, and the behaviour matches the dissociation. Table~\ref{tab:main} and
Figs.~\ref{fig:training}--\ref{fig:multiseed} summarise the multi-seed results against the
CheXpert label. Both CXR models beat the triage baselines by more than 12 AUROC points, which
confirms that the radiograph carries the signal that discriminates. Adding triage
vitals leaves discrimination within the pre-specified margin: across seeds the paired
concat$-$image $\Delta$AUROC is $+0.004\pm0.009$ (range $[-0.007,+0.014]$). A hierarchical
bootstrap that resamples both the training seed and the patients, so that it reflects the
variance a single deployed model would show, gives a 95\% CI of $[-0.016,+0.022]$; this
includes zero and lies entirely inside the pre-specified $\pm0.05$ equivalence margin, so
the two one-sided tests reject and concat fusion is statistically equivalent to, and not
significantly different from, the image model. The comparison is adequately powered: the
smallest AUROC difference detectable at 80\% power is $0.029$, still below the margin, so
the equivalence is not an artefact of low power. Equivalence here means that any effect is
smaller than $\pm0.05$, not that it is exactly zero. The result does not depend on our choice of
feature-level fusion either. A late-fusion baseline in the style of MedFuse, which
combines the separately trained image and triage models with a logistic meta-learner
fitted on the validation split, is also neutral ($\Delta$AUROC $+0.000\pm0.001$ across
seeds; TOST again confirms equivalence), even though the meta-learner gives the triage
stream a real weight. No fusion we tried, whether early or late, recovers a gain in
discrimination. Attention fusion matches the image AUROC but is badly and unevenly
calibrated (ECE $0.076\pm0.042$; Fig.~\ref{fig:deltas}), a reminder that AUROC alone is
not enough for choosing a model. The precision-recall curves (Fig.~\ref{fig:pr}) follow
the same ordering at the 45.3\% prevalence. The result also shows in the
representations: in a t-SNE of the learned embeddings (Fig.~\ref{fig:tsne}), the triage
embedding is not aligned with the label and the fused representation is essentially the
image embedding.

\paragraph{Training dynamics.} Figure~\ref{fig:training} shows the fine-tuning curves.
All three models reach their best validation AUPRC within the first one or two epochs,
which reflects the strong pretrained initialisation (ImageNet then CheXpert) and the low
backbone learning rate: little needs to change for the model to perform near its ceiling.
The curves also show where the models struggle. The image-only model overfits, its
training loss falling toward zero while validation loss climbs steadily and validation
AUPRC decays after the second epoch, so early stopping is essential. The attention fusion
model converges earliest but is the least stable: its validation loss rises sharply (to
about 2.9) and its validation AUPRC oscillates from epoch to epoch, consistent with the
poor and variable calibration it shows in Table~\ref{tab:main}. Selecting on validation
AUPRC stops each model before these failure modes take over.

carry large worst-bin errors (MCE up to 0.489), so they can be badly wrong for some
patients even when their mean calibration looks good. These differences matter in
the emergency department: an overconfident model can attach falsely reassuring low
probabilities to genuine pneumonia, while a badly under-confident model inflates apparent
risk and erodes trust in the tool, so calibration, and not discrimination alone, has to
be checked before any deployment.

\emph{Caution 1: a single run inflates the calibration effect.} The canonical fine-tuned
checkpoints (the models behind our headline numbers, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:reliability})
suggested the concat model was markedly better calibrated than the image model (ECE 0.040
vs 0.067, a 40\% gap). That single-checkpoint comparison does not survive replication. The
image checkpoint's ECE of 0.067 in fact sits at the high (worse) end of what training
stochasticity produces: across five seeds the image-only ECE ranges 0.041--0.060 (mean
0.053) and the concat ECE 0.023--0.056 (mean 0.040), so the paired advantage shrinks to
$\Delta$ECE $-0.013\pm0.016$ (4/5 seeds favour concat; the range crosses zero). A single
run therefore overstated the benefit by roughly twofold, and the specific checkpoint used
happened to land among the least-calibrated image runs, which is itself the caution in
miniature. Within-checkpoint uncertainty tells the same
story: patient-level bootstrap 95\% CIs on the seed-averaged ECE overlap almost
entirely (image 0.042, CI $[0.033,0.077]$; concat 0.037, CI $[0.028,0.068]$), so the
calibration gap is not resolvable even before seed variance is added. The apparent gap is also binning-dependent
(Fig.~\ref{fig:ece}): under coarser or quantile bins it shrinks further
\citep{nixon2019measuring}. Brier scores are essentially unchanged, so any effect is
distributional alignment rather than refinement. The attention fusion model, by
contrast, is the most variable of all: its ECE ranges from 0.029 to 0.144 across seeds
(mean $0.076\pm0.042$), better calibrated than the image model in one run and far worse
in another, so its miscalibration is unstable rather than a fixed property of the design.
A simple post-hoc rescaling does not explain the single-checkpoint gap either: temperature
scaling of the image model (optimal $T=1.21$, fit on the validation logits) leaves its
test ECE essentially unchanged (0.067 to 0.065, and no better on a patient-level
bootstrap), so the apparent gap is not a scale mismatch that recalibration removes. The
lesson of Caution~1 is therefore not that the difference is trivially fixable but that it
does not survive replication: a single checkpoint suggests a large, robust-looking
calibration advantage that averaging over seeds reveals to be fragile~\citep{ovadia2019trust}.

\subsection{Caution 2: the ``labs'' benefit is missing-data structure}
\label{subsec:labs}
Appending early labs gives the only seed-consistent AUROC gain (labs$-$image
$+0.009\pm0.005$, 5/5 seeds, range excluding zero). Three analyses show this is
neither chemistry nor leakage. \emph{Ablation:} a model using laboratory
\emph{missingness flags only} (no values) reproduces the gain exactly (flags$-$image
$+0.009$; Fig.~\ref{fig:deltas}); the lab values add nothing further to
discrimination. \emph{Subcohort:} on the 269 test studies that actually have labs,
discrimination is tied (labs 0.778 vs image 0.776). \emph{Leakage audit:} 0 of
314{,}145 lab measurements post-$t_0$; 0 cross-split subjects; preprocessing fit on
train only; the ``any lab drawn'' indicator has near-null univariate AUROC (0.464).
The gain therefore reflects the weak complementary signal of \emph{whether} bloodwork
was ordered, is small, and lies within the patient-level bootstrap CI.

\subsection{Clinical availability of laboratory results}
\label{subsec:labcov}
Under time-safe matching, only about a quarter of ED-anchored CXR studies have any
resolved laboratory value at or before imaging (25.4\% of the full cohort; 272 of
the 1{,}075 test studies, 25.3\%, of which 269 have a radiograph on disk and form the
lab-present evaluation subcohort; Fig.~\ref{fig:labcov}).
Complete-blood-count and metabolic panels reach $\sim$25\%, but the most
pneumonia-specific markers (CRP and procalcitonin) are present in only 0.5\% and
0.6\% of studies. At the moment of imaging the informative labs are essentially
unavailable, which explains both the small labs effect and its missingness origin.

\subsection{Interpretability}
\label{subsec:interp}
For the triage-only XGBoost model, exact TreeSHAP attributes the largest importance
to SpO$_2$ (mean $|\text{SHAP}|=0.148$), followed by heart rate (0.099), temperature
(0.078) and respiratory rate (0.062) (Table~\ref{tab:shap};
Fig.~\ref{fig:shap}), consistent with the pathophysiology of pneumonia, in which
alveolar consolidation impairs gas exchange. For the image branch we compare six
class-activation-mapping variants, CAM~\citep{zhou2016cam},
Grad-CAM~\citep{selvaraju2017gradcam}, Grad-CAM++~\citep{chattopadhyay2018gradcampp},
Score-CAM~\citep{wang2020scorecam}, Smooth Grad-CAM++~\citep{omeiza2019smoothgradcampp}
and XGrad-CAM~\citep{fu2020xgradcam}, on confident true positives
(Fig.~\ref{fig:gradcam}). The methods largely agree with one another and avoid the image
borders and hardware markers, so the model is not keying on obvious artefacts. Their
focus, however, sits on the central chest, the perihilar and mediastinal region together
with the medial lung, rather than tightly on the peripheral opacity a radiologist would
mark. The variants differ mainly in sharpness, with CAM, Grad-CAM, XGrad-CAM and
Grad-CAM++ the most focal and Smooth Grad-CAM++ the most diffuse, but all share this
central emphasis. This matches reports that saliency maps for chest-radiograph
classifiers often localise the abnormality poorly~\citep{arun2021saliency}: the maps show
roughly where the model looks, not a reliable outline of disease, and are better read as
a coarse sanity check than as evidence of clinically precise reasoning. The central focus
does raise the question of whether the model leans on non-pathological cues rather than
the consolidation itself, a well-documented failure mode for chest-radiograph
classifiers~\citep{zech2018variable,degrave2021shortcuts}. The strongest evidence against
a purely spurious signal here is that the image model transfers to a different institution
(AUROC 0.722 on NIH, Section~\ref{subsec:ext}), which a model relying on dataset-specific
artefacts would not do; the pneumonia-detection failure that \citet{zech2018variable}
reported was precisely a failure of cross-site transfer, so our external result speaks
directly to that concern. The imperfect localisation nonetheless remains a reason to treat
these saliency maps as weak evidence.

\subsection{Modern backbone and external validation}
\label{subsec:ext}
A CheXpert-trained DenseNet-121 \citep{cohen2022torchxrayvision}, used zero-shot
(AUROC 0.667) or as a frozen-feature linear probe (0.687), does not outperform our
task-tuned model (0.737), indicating the conclusions are not an artefact of an
outdated backbone. To test whether fusion neutrality is instead specific to the CNN
architecture or to feature freezing, we also fine-tuned a Vision Transformer
(ViT-Small/16, ImageNet-pretrained) end-to-end on the identical cohort and split
(seed 42): image-only reached AUROC 0.712 and triage-concat fusion 0.696, a change of
${-}0.015$. A contemporary transformer backbone, fully fine-tuned, therefore also
extracts no discriminative synergy from triage vitals over imaging alone, so the
neutrality holds across both backbone family and training regime.
On external validation against all 112{,}120 NIH ChestX-ray14
radiographs \citep{wang2017chestxray} (a different institution, scanner population and
label pipeline; prevalence 1.28\%), discrimination transfers with minimal loss:
AUROC $0.722\pm0.005$ across five seeds (ensemble 0.726), only 0.016 below the
internal 0.737 (Fig.~\ref{fig:external}). As expected under the large prevalence shift
($45\%\to1.3\%$), prevalence-dependent metrics degrade (AUPRC 0.030, ECE 0.44)
because probabilities are calibrated to the internal base rate; AUROC, which is
prevalence-independent, is the appropriate cross-dataset metric, and it holds despite
domain shift and NLP-derived external labels.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
The value of adding clinical context to a chest-radiograph model is not a fixed property
of the data; it depends on the label the model is asked to predict. For a fixed image model
in this ED cohort, the same triage vitals leave discrimination unchanged against the
radiographic reading of the film but improve it against the clinically defined diagnosis,
and the difference between the two effects is significant. The same contrast appears for
heart failure, though that condition is too small to settle on its own. This resolves an
apparent contradiction in the literature: fusion has repeatedly helped for prognostic and
outcome targets and repeatedly looked unhelpful for radiographic findings. Both can be true
at once, because those studies differ not only in task and architecture but in where their
label comes from.

The reason is visible in the vitals themselves. The triage vitals that matter for the
clinical label are the systemic signs of infection, fever and overall acuity, which do not
appear as a radiographic
opacity, and a pairwise decomposition shows the vitals are redundant for the radiographic
sign but complementary for the diagnosis. A report-derived label is close to a reading of the
same image the model already sees, so clinical context has little room to add. A diagnosis
recorded independently of the image rests partly on physiology the film does not carry, so
the same context becomes useful. That signal is not exclusively physiological, though: an
image model retrained on the clinical label recovers much of it
(Section~\ref{subsec:earlyfusion}), so triage is one route to what the radiographic-trained
model misses rather than the only one. The discrepancy between report labels and image labels
is itself documented~\citep{jain2021visualchexbert}; what we add is that it can decide the
conclusion of a multimodal study.

Two further findings carry beyond this task. First, a calibration advantage that
looks large in a single run (a 40\% reduction in ECE) is roughly halved and becomes
unstable once we average over five seeds; since calibration is decisive for clinical use
yet easily overstated \citep{vancalster2019calibration}, claims from one run should be read
with caution and replication across seeds ought to be routine. Second, when
clinical inputs are missing often and not at random, a fusion model can exploit the
pattern of missingness and show a benefit that reflects neither the measured values
(the subcohort with labs present shows no effect of the values) nor leakage (the
temporal, split and preprocessing audits are clean). The ablation that keeps only the
missingness indicators makes this mechanism explicit, and to our knowledge it is
rarely reported in clinical multimodal studies. Both cautions are easy and cheap to
apply.

\paragraph{A cognitive-computation view.} The finding restates a familiar principle of
evidence integration: the value of a cue depends on the inference it serves. A clinician
weighs the same vital signs differently when asking what the film shows and when asking what
the patient has, and the models behave the same way. Fusion helps when the target draws on
information the image does not carry, and adds nothing when the target is the image itself.
The two cautions sit at the same level. Calibrated confidence has to be estimated across runs
rather than read off one, and a model that treats the presence of a measurement as evidence,
as clinicians sometimes do, must not confuse that ordering signal with the physiology it
stands for. Both are questions about how a system should combine evidence and how far its
stated confidence can be trusted, which is where cognitively motivated decision support has
to operate.

\paragraph{A practical guide for multimodal studies.} The result gives a concrete
instruction: state where the target label comes from, and expect the value of fusion to
follow it. Fusion of the radiograph with clinical data has worked best for prognostic and
screening targets whose ground truth is not the image itself, such as ICU
mortality~\citep{hayat2022medfuse}, inpatient outcomes~\citep{soenksen2022multimodal} and
screening for acute heart failure in the ED~\citep{lee2024multimodal}, and a recent benchmark
finds the gains concentrate in phenotypes that need complementary
information~\citep{yin2026multimodal}. A report-derived radiographic label sits at the other
end: the image already accounts for it. A study that reports fusion as useless against such a
label, without noting that the label is read from the image, risks discouraging fusion for
the clinical targets where it does help. The reverse error is equally available, since a
label that leaks the diagnosis into the inputs can make fusion look better than it is. Naming
the label's provenance guards against both.

\paragraph{On the moderate absolute performance.} The absolute AUROC against the radiographic
label (about 0.74) is moderate, which is expected: pneumonia is among the CheXpert findings
with the lowest reader agreement, below 40\% expert agreement in the CheXpert
study~\citep{irvin2019chexpert}, so part of the ceiling is genuine label noise rather than
limited model capacity. That noise affects every model equally and cancels in the paired
comparison, so it does not weaken either the radiographic equivalence or the clinical gain.

\paragraph{Comparison with related work.} Earlier multimodal CXR studies typically
report gains from a single run on ICU or inpatient
cohorts~\citep{hayat2022medfuse,soenksen2022multimodal,lee2024multimodal}, and evaluate
against a single label. VisualCheXbert treats the report-versus-image label discrepancy as a
labelling problem to be corrected~\citep{jain2021visualchexbert}; the CareBench benchmark uses
clinical labels throughout and reports where fusion helps, but does not contrast label
provenance directly~\citep{yin2026multimodal}. We take the discrepancy as the experimental
variable and show that holding everything else fixed while changing the label flips the
conclusion. This has practical teeth: studies of CXR pneumonia fusion routinely benchmark
against report-derived labels, so a null result in that setting may reflect the label rather
than the modality, and our contrast shows the sign can change with the label alone. Our
missingness result complements the literature on informative
missingness: where GRU-D and similar models \emph{use} missingness as a predictive
feature~\citep{che2018recurrent}, we show that in a fusion model the same signal can be
mistaken for a benefit from the laboratory values, and we give a simple control,
keeping only the flags, that separates the two. Our calibration result agrees with
benchmarks showing that the uncertainty of a single model is unreliable under
distribution shift, whereas averaging several models is more robust~\citep{ovadia2019trust}.
Comparing raw numbers across different cohorts and label definitions is not informative;
the contribution is the rigour of the evaluation and the two cautions.

\paragraph{Limitations.} Training and the multimodal evaluation use a single
institution (MIMIC) and are retrospective. The external validation confirms that the
image model generalises to NIH, but it is limited to imaging: to our knowledge no
public dataset links ED triage, or early labs, to the chest radiograph, so the fusion
models themselves cannot yet be validated externally, and building such a linked
multi-site resource is an important community need. The clinical label has its own
caveat: an ICD diagnosis assigned during the encounter may be informed in part by the
radiograph. This works against our result rather than for it, since image information in the
label raises the image-only baseline and leaves less room for triage to add, so it shrinks
the clinical gain rather than inflating it. The pre-imaging nature of the triage vitals is a
separate safeguard, because they cannot encode the report, and the effect persists when the
clinical stream is restricted to pre-imaging chief-complaint symptoms. A second caveat
is that the clinical gain is not distributed equally: at a fixed operating threshold,
fusion lowers the true-positive rate for Black patients even as it raises their AUROC, a
threshold-calibration effect that would need per-subgroup operating points before
deployment. The subgroup counts are small, so this is a caution to act on rather than a
firm estimate. The sharp fall in
prevalence-dependent calibration on NIH (from 45\% to 1.3\%) is expected rather than a
defect: before deployment in a population with a different base rate, a recalibration
fitted on a small labelled sample from the target site (temperature scaling for sharpness
together with an intercept or prior correction for the base-rate shift) would be needed to
restore calibrated probabilities without retraining; a single temperature is not
sufficient on its own, as our internal check left the image ECE essentially unchanged. The subcohort
with labs present is small ($n{=}269$), but this reflects the clinical reality that
laboratory results are rarely available at the moment of the first ED radiograph
(Section~\ref{subsec:labcov}) rather than a sampling artefact, and it is the right
denominator for the question of whether labs help when they are available. The
\emph{u\_ignore} policy also raises the internal prevalence to 45.3\%, higher than a
typical ED pneumonia base rate; this is a property of the labelled evaluation subset
rather than a population estimate, and because AUROC is prevalence-independent and the
external validation probes a very different base rate (1.3\%), the discrimination
conclusion does not depend on it, while the prevalence-dependent metrics (AUPRC, PPV)
should be read at the local deployment prevalence. The NIH labels are text-mined, which
adds noise to the external estimate. Finally, we did not run an exhaustive hyperparameter
search over the fusion branch; the neutrality is nonetheless unlikely to reflect
under-tuning, since it holds across two feature-level fusion designs (concatenation and
attention), a decision-level late-fusion baseline whose meta-learner gives the triage
stream a real weight, restricted and expanded tabular feature sets (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}),
and the addition of a full early-laboratory panel, none of which recovers a discriminative
gain.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
Whether the clinical context available at triage improves a chest-radiograph model
depends on the provenance of the label. Under a verified timing constraint and
replication across seeds, adding triage vitals to a fixed image model does not improve
discrimination against the radiographic reading of the film, but it does improve
discrimination against the clinically defined pneumonia diagnosis, and the gap between the
two effects is significant; heart failure shows the same direction on a smaller sample. The
gain is carried by
physiology the radiograph does not show, chiefly fever and acuity, and it is
complementary rather than redundant. A label read from the image can therefore make
useful clinical context look worthless, so studies of multimodal fusion should state
where their label comes from. The image model itself is robust and generalises across
institutions with an AUROC drop of only 0.016, and the study contributes a reproducible,
time-safe multimodal ED pipeline together with two reusable cautions, calibration
inflation from a single run and missingness as signal. Future work includes a broader
survey of conditions as linked resources allow, validation across sites, and prospective
evaluation with per-subgroup recalibration to the local base rate.
